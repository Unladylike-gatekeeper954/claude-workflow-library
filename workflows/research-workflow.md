# Research Workflow

> Go from a vague question to a well-organized, source-backed research document — step by step.

---

## Who Is This For?

- Students writing research papers or thesis projects
- Professionals researching a market, competitor, or trend
- Curious people who want to deeply understand any topic
- Content creators who need accurate, well-sourced information

## What You'll Have at the End

- A clearly defined research question
- A broad overview of the topic landscape
- Deep, organized findings on each subtopic
- A list of key sources and where to find them
- A final research summary you can use immediately

---

## The Workflow

### Step 1 — Define Your Research Question

Vague questions get vague answers. This step sharpens your question so every step after this is focused and useful.

**Copy this prompt and fill in the `[BRACKETS]`:**

```
Role: You are a research advisor who helps people turn
vague ideas into focused, answerable research questions.

Objective: Help me turn my rough topic into a clear,
specific research question.

Context:
- My rough topic is: [YOUR TOPIC — e.g., "how social media
  affects teenagers" or "best CRM tools for startups"]
- This research is for: [essay / work project / business
  decision / personal curiosity / content creation]
- My audience is: [who will read this — professor, boss,
  clients, myself]
- How deep I need to go: [surface overview / moderate depth /
  very detailed and thorough]

Output:
1. Rewrite my rough topic as 3 different focused research
   questions (from narrow to broad)
2. For each question, tell me what kind of information I'd
   need to answer it
3. Recommend which question is best for my situation and why
```

**Pick one question from the output and use it for the rest of the workflow.**

---

### Step 2 — Get a Bird's-Eye View

Before going deep, understand the full landscape. This prevents you from missing important angles.

```
Role: You are a research analyst who gives clear,
structured overviews of complex topics.

Objective: Give me a complete overview of this topic so
I understand the key areas before diving deeper.

Context:
- My research question is: [YOUR QUESTION FROM STEP 1]
- I currently know: [what you already know — even if it's
  "very little"]
- I need this for: [PURPOSE]

Output: Give me:
1. A brief summary of the topic (5-6 sentences, plain
   language)
2. The 5-7 main subtopics or themes I should explore
3. Key terms I should know (with simple definitions)
4. Any major debates or disagreements in this area
5. What's changed recently about this topic (last 1-2 years)

Keep everything jargon-free unless a technical term is
essential — and if so, define it.
```

---

### Step 3 — Deep Dive into Each Subtopic

Now go through each subtopic from Step 2, one at a time. This is where the real learning happens.

```
Role: You are a thorough researcher who presents findings
in a clear, balanced, and organized way.

Objective: Give me a detailed breakdown of [SUBTOPIC]
as it relates to my research question.

Context:
- My research question: [YOUR QUESTION]
- The subtopic I'm exploring now: [SUBTOPIC FROM STEP 2]
- Depth needed: [moderate / very detailed]
- I want to use this information in: [paper / report /
  presentation / decision / article]

Output: Cover these points:
1. What is this subtopic and why does it matter to my
   question?
2. What are the key facts, data points, or findings?
3. What do experts or sources say about this? (Name
   specific studies, reports, or people if you can)
4. Are there different perspectives or disagreements?
5. How does this connect to the other subtopics in my
   research?

Keep it structured with clear headings. Aim for 300-500
words.
```

**Repeat this for every subtopic from Step 2.**

**Helpful follow-ups:**

- `What are the most credible sources I should read about this?`
- `What data or statistics exist on this topic?`
- `What would someone who disagrees with this say?`

---

### Step 4 — Find and Verify Sources

AI can point you to sources, but you should verify them. This step helps you build a real source list.

```
Role: You are a research librarian who helps people find
credible, relevant sources.

Objective: Help me find real, verifiable sources for my
research.

Context:
- My research question: [YOUR QUESTION]
- Subtopics I've covered: [LIST THEM]
- Type of sources I need: [academic papers / news articles /
  industry reports / government data / books / all of the above]
- I need sources that are: [recent (last 2 years) / classic
  foundational works / a mix of both]

Output:
1. For each subtopic, suggest 2-3 specific sources I can
   look up (give titles, author names, publication names,
   or URLs where possible)
2. Tell me where to find each one (Google Scholar, specific
   websites, databases)
3. Flag which sources are most important vs. nice-to-have
4. Warn me about any types of sources to avoid for this
   topic (biased outlets, outdated data, etc.)
```

**Important:** Always double-check that the sources AI suggests actually exist. Search for them yourself. AI sometimes generates plausible-sounding sources that aren't real.

---

### Step 5 — Synthesize Everything into a Summary

Now pull it all together into something usable.

```
Role: You are a senior research analyst who writes clear,
well-organized research summaries.

Objective: Combine all my research findings into one
coherent, well-structured summary.

Context:
- My research question: [YOUR QUESTION]
- Key findings from my subtopics:
  [PASTE KEY POINTS FROM EACH SUBTOPIC — or say "based on
  our entire conversation above"]
- This summary will be used for: [paper / report /
  presentation / decision making / content creation]
- Target length: [500 words / 1000 words / 2000 words]
- Tone: [academic / professional / conversational]

Output: Write a research summary that includes:
1. Introduction — what question we set out to answer
2. Key Findings — the main things we discovered,
   organized by theme
3. Analysis — what the findings mean, patterns or
   connections between them
4. Gaps — what we still don't know or couldn't confirm
5. Conclusion — a clear answer to the research question
   (or the best answer given what we found)
6. Recommended Next Steps — what to do with this research

Use clear section headings. Write in [TONE]. No filler
or repetition.
```

## Pro Tips

- **Start a fresh conversation for each major subtopic** if your research is complex. Long conversations can cause the AI to lose context.
- **Challenge the AI.** Ask *"What's the opposing viewpoint?"* and *"What might be wrong about this?"* to get balanced research.
- **Save as you go.** Copy each step's output into a document so you don't lose anything.
- **Don't trust sources blindly.** Always search for any source the AI mentions to make sure it's real and says what the AI claims.
- **Use AI for structure, use search engines for verification.** The AI is great at organizing and analyzing — but always cross-check facts.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Hurts You | Do This Instead |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Starting without a clear question | Your research goes in 10 directions | Spend time on Step 1 to focus your question |
| Taking AI sources at face value | Some suggested sources don't exist | Always verify every source with a real search |
| Skipping the overview | You miss important angles | Do Step 2 before diving deep |
| Only looking at one side | Your research is biased | Always ask for opposing viewpoints |
| Not saving outputs | You lose work and context | Copy each step into a running document |

---

## Quick Start

Short on time? Here's the minimum path:

1. Do **Step 1** to focus your question
2. Do **Step 2** to see the landscape
3. Do **Step 3** for your 2-3 most important subtopics
4. Do **Step 5** to get a summary
5. Come back to Steps 3-4 if you need to go deeper
