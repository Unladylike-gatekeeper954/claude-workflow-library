# Research Workflow

> Go from a vague question to a well-organized, source-backed research document — step by step.

---

## Who Is This For?

- Students writing research papers or thesis projects
- Professionals researching a market, competitor, or trend
- Curious people who want to deeply understand any topic
- Content creators who need accurate, well-sourced information

## What You'll Have at the End

- A clearly defined research question
- A broad overview of the topic landscape
- Deep, organized findings on each subtopic
- A list of key sources and where to find them
- A final research summary you can use immediately

---

## The Workflow

### Step 1 — Define Your Research Question

Vague questions get vague answers. This step sharpens your question so every step after this is focused and useful.

**Copy this prompt and fill in the `[BRACKETS]`:**
```
Role: You are a research advisor who specializes in
helping people turn vague ideas into focused, answerable
research questions — especially people without a formal
research background.

Objective: Help me turn my rough topic into a clear,
specific research question I can actually investigate.

Context:
- My rough topic is: [YOUR TOPIC]
- This research is for: [essay / work project / business
  decision / personal curiosity / content creation]
- My audience is: [who will read this]
- How deep I need to go: [surface overview / moderate
  depth / very detailed and thorough]

Output:
1. Rewrite my rough topic as 3 different focused research
   questions — from narrow to broad
2. For each question, tell me what kind of information
   I would need to answer it
3. Recommend which question best fits my situation and why

Rules:
- Make the questions specific enough that I know exactly
  what to look for
- If my topic is too vague to work with, tell me what
  I need to clarify before proceeding
- Do not recommend the most complex question just because
  it sounds impressive — match the question to my purpose
```

**Pick one question from the output and use it for the rest of the workflow.**

---

### Step 2 — Get a Bird's-Eye View

Before going deep, understand the full landscape. This prevents you from missing important angles.
```
Role: You are a research analyst who gives clear,
structured overviews of complex topics to people who
are new to the subject.

Objective: Give me a complete overview of this topic so
I understand the key areas before diving deeper.

Context:
- My research question is: [YOUR QUESTION FROM STEP 1]
- I currently know: [what you already know]
- I need this for: [PURPOSE]

Output:
1. A brief summary of the topic — 5-6 sentences, plain language
2. The 5-7 main subtopics or themes I should explore
3. Key terms I should know with simple definitions
4. Any major debates or disagreements in this area
5. What has changed recently about this topic

Rules:
- Keep everything jargon-free unless a technical term
  is essential — define it immediately if so
- If there are common misconceptions, flag them now
- Do not bury the most important subtopics at the end —
  lead with what matters most
```

---

### Step 3 — Deep Dive into Each Subtopic

Now go through each subtopic from Step 2, one at a time.
```
Role: You are a thorough researcher who presents findings
in a clear, balanced, and organized way — specifically
for non-specialist audiences who need to understand and
use this information.

Objective: Give me a detailed breakdown of [SUBTOPIC]
as it relates to my research question.

Context:
- My research question: [YOUR QUESTION]
- The subtopic I am exploring now: [SUBTOPIC FROM STEP 2]
- Depth needed: [moderate / very detailed]
- I want to use this information in: [paper / report /
  presentation / decision / article]

Output:
1. What is this subtopic and why does it matter?
2. What are the key facts, data points, or findings?
3. What do experts or sources say about this?
4. Are there different perspectives or disagreements?
5. How does this connect to the other subtopics?

Rules:
- Use clear headings for each point
- Aim for 300-500 words
- If you reference a specific study or source, name it
  so I can look it up
- If a subtopic has genuinely conflicting evidence,
  present both sides — do not pick one and ignore the other
```

**Repeat this for every subtopic from Step 2.**

**Helpful follow-ups:**
- `What are the most credible sources I should read about this?`
- `What would someone who disagrees with this say?`
- `What data or statistics exist on this topic?`

---

### Step 4 — Find and Verify Sources

AI can point you to sources, but you should always verify them yourself.
```
Role: You are a research librarian who helps people
find credible, relevant sources — and teaches them
how to evaluate what they find.

Objective: Help me find real, verifiable sources for
my research and understand how to assess their quality.

Context:
- My research question: [YOUR QUESTION]
- Subtopics I have covered: [LIST THEM]
- Type of sources I need: [academic papers / news
  articles / industry reports / government data /
  books / all of the above]
- I need sources that are: [recent / foundational /
  a mix of both]

Output:
1. For each subtopic, suggest 2-3 specific sources —
   titles, author names, publication names, or URLs
2. Where to find each one
3. Which sources are most important versus nice-to-have
4. Types of sources to avoid for this topic and why

Rules:
- Only suggest sources you are confident exist — if
  uncertain, say so clearly
- Do not invent plausible-sounding citations
- Prioritize sources accessible without a university
  library subscription
```

**Important:** Always verify that sources AI suggests actually exist. Search for them yourself.

---

### Step 5 — Synthesize Everything into a Summary

Pull it all together into something usable.
```
Role: You are a senior research analyst who writes
clear, well-organized research summaries for busy
professionals and students who need to act on findings.

Objective: Combine all my research findings into one
coherent, well-structured summary I can use immediately.

Context:
- My research question: [YOUR QUESTION]
- Key findings from my subtopics: [PASTE KEY POINTS
  or "based on our entire conversation above"]
- This summary will be used for: [paper / report /
  presentation / decision / content]
- Target length: [500 / 1000 / 2000 words]
- Tone: [academic / professional / conversational]

Output:
1. Introduction — what question we set out to answer
2. Key Findings — main things discovered, organized by theme
3. Analysis — what the findings mean and patterns between them
4. Gaps — what we still do not know or could not confirm
5. Conclusion — the clearest answer to the research question
6. Recommended Next Steps — what to do with this research

Rules:
- No filler or repetition — every sentence must add something new
- If the research does not support a clear conclusion, say so
- Write in the stated tone consistently throughout
- Keep section headings clear so I can navigate quickly
```

## Pro Tips

- **Start a fresh conversation for each major subtopic** if your research is complex — long conversations can cause the AI to lose context.
- **Challenge the AI.** Ask "What is the opposing viewpoint?" to get balanced research.
- **Save as you go.** Copy each step's output into a document so you do not lose anything.
- **Do not trust sources blindly.** Always verify any source the AI mentions.
- **Use AI for structure, search engines for verification.**

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Hurts You | Do This Instead |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Starting without a clear question | Your research goes in 10 directions | Spend time on Step 1 to focus your question |
| Taking AI sources at face value | Some suggested sources do not exist | Always verify every source with a real search |
| Skipping the overview | You miss important angles | Do Step 2 before diving deep |
| Only looking at one side | Your research is biased | Always ask for opposing viewpoints |
| Not saving outputs | You lose work and context | Copy each step into a running document |

---

## Quick Start

Short on time? Here is the minimum path:

1. Do **Step 1** to focus your question
2. Do **Step 2** to see the landscape
3. Do **Step 3** for your 2-3 most important subtopics
4. Do **Step 5** to get a summary
5. Come back to Steps 3-4 if you need to go deeper
