# Business Workflow

> Go from a rough business idea to a clear plan with market research, a business model, and actionable next steps — step by step.

---

## Who Is This For?

- First-time entrepreneurs exploring a business idea
- Freelancers looking to turn their skills into a business
- Small business owners rethinking their strategy
- Anyone who wants to validate an idea before investing time or money

## What You'll Have at the End

- A clear, refined business idea
- Market research showing who your customers are and what competitors exist
- A simple business model showing how you will make money
- Basic financial projections
- A 30-day action plan to get started

---

## The Workflow

### Step 1 — Clarify and Sharpen Your Idea

Most business ideas are too vague. This step forces you to get specific.

**Copy this prompt and fill in the `[BRACKETS]`:**
```
Role: You are a startup advisor who has helped hundreds
of first-time founders refine their ideas — you are
known for asking the questions people are avoiding
and telling them what they need to hear, not what
they want to hear.

Objective: Help me turn my rough business idea into a
clear, specific concept I can actually test.

Context:
- My idea: [DESCRIBE YOUR IDEA]
- Problem I am trying to solve: [WHAT PROBLEM or
  "I am not sure yet"]
- Who I think my customer is: [WHO or "not sure yet"]
- My relevant skills or background: [WHAT YOU ARE GOOD AT]
- My starting budget: [low under $500 / medium $500-$5000 /
  high $5000+]

Output:
1. My idea rewritten in one clear sentence
2. The specific problem this solves and who has it
3. What makes this different from what already exists
4. 3 things that could make this idea succeed
5. 3 things that could make this idea fail
6. The one question I must answer before going further

Rules:
- Do not encourage the idea just to be supportive —
  if there is a serious flaw, name it clearly
- If the idea is too vague to assess, ask me the
  specific questions you need before proceeding
- Do not assume my target customer — if I have not
  defined them, flag it
```

### Step 2 — Research Your Market

Know who your customers are, how big the opportunity is, and who you are competing with.
```
Role: You are a market research analyst who translates
complex market data into plain, actionable language for
entrepreneurs without a business school background.

Objective: Give me a clear picture of the market for
my business idea so I know if the opportunity is real.

Context:
- My business idea: [ONE-SENTENCE VERSION FROM STEP 1]
- My target customer: [FROM STEP 1]
- Industry or category: [e.g., fitness, education,
  e-commerce, professional services]
- Location: [local / national / global / online only]

Output:
1. Target Customer Profile:
   - Who they are
   - What they currently do to solve this problem
   - How much they spend on solutions like this
   - Where they spend time online and offline
2. Market Size:
   - Growing, stable, or shrinking?
   - Roughly how many potential customers exist?
3. Competitor Analysis:
   - 3-5 competitors or similar solutions
   - What each does well and poorly
   - Approximate pricing
4. Key Insight:
   - The one gap my idea could fill

Rules:
- Be honest about market size — do not inflate it
- If I have no real differentiation, say so directly
- If a competitor dominates this space, name them and
  explain what it would take to compete
```

**After getting the analysis, ask:**
```
Based on this market research, what is the biggest
risk with my idea? And what is the biggest opportunity
I should focus on?
```

### Step 3 — Build Your Business Model

This is how you will actually make money.
```
Role: You are a business model expert who helps first-
time entrepreneurs build simple, realistic revenue plans
— you know that most new businesses fail not because
the idea is bad but because the economics do not work.

Objective: Help me create a clear business model that
shows how my business will actually generate revenue.

Context:
- My business idea: [ONE-SENTENCE VERSION]
- My target customer: [FROM STEP 2]
- What competitors charge: [FROM STEP 2]
- My starting budget: [low / medium / high]
- How I want to start: [side project / full-time /
  not sure yet]

Output:
1. Revenue Model:
   - Pricing structure and how to charge
   - Why this pricing makes sense for my customer
   - Suggested starting price
2. Cost Structure:
   - One-time startup costs
   - Monthly ongoing costs
   - What I can delay until I have revenue
3. Customer Acquisition:
   - 3 ways to find my first 10 customers
   - 2 ways to scale beyond that
4. Simple Value Statement:
   - What I deliver, how, what the customer pays —
     in one sentence

Rules:
- Do not suggest a pricing model my customer cannot afford
- Flag immediately if my cost structure makes
  profitability very difficult
- Keep the model simple enough to explain to anyone
```

### Step 4 — Create Basic Financial Projections

Simple math that shows whether this can work.
```
Role: You are a financial advisor for small and micro
businesses who makes numbers accessible to people with
no finance background — you believe founders who
understand their numbers make better decisions.

Objective: Create basic financial projections that show
whether my business can be profitable and when.

Context:
- My pricing: [FROM STEP 3]
- My monthly costs: [FROM STEP 3]
- My startup costs: [FROM STEP 3]
- Customers I can realistically get in month 1:
  [YOUR ESTIMATE or "give me a realistic estimate"]
- Growth rate I am hoping for: [e.g., "10 new customers
  per month" or "suggest a realistic rate"]

Output:
1. Monthly table for 12 months:
   | Month | Customers | Revenue | Costs | Profit/Loss |
2. Break-Even Point:
   - How many customers to break even
   - How many months that will likely take
3. Three Scenarios:
   | Scenario | Customers | Revenue | Notes |
   Best case, realistic, worst case
4. Key Metrics to Track:
   - The 3 numbers I should watch every week

Rules:
- Explain each number in plain language
- If projections show I cannot break even within 12
  months, say so clearly
- Use conservative assumptions by default
```

### Step 5 — Build Your 30-Day Action Plan

Ideas fail because people do not take action. This gives you a clear path forward.
```
Role: You are a business launch coach who specializes
in helping people take their first real steps without
overthinking — imperfect action beats perfect planning
every time.

Objective: Create a realistic 30-day action plan to
start making real progress on my business.

Context:
- My business idea: [ONE-SENTENCE VERSION]
- My current situation: [employed / freelancer /
  student / unemployed / other]
- Hours I can dedicate per week: [NUMBER]
- Budget for the first month: [AMOUNT]
- Skills I have: [LIST]
- Skills I need but do not have: [LIST]
- My biggest fear or blocker: [WHAT IS STOPPING YOU]

Output:
Week 1 — Validate:
- 3 specific actions to confirm people want this
  before building anything

Week 2 — Build:
- 3 specific actions to create the simplest version
  of my product or service

Week 3 — Launch:
- 3 specific actions to get my first paying customer

Week 4 — Learn:
- 3 specific actions to gather feedback and decide
  what to do next

For each action: exactly what to do, how long it
takes, what free tools to use, what done looks like.

Rules:
- Do not suggest actions requiring skills or budget
  I do not have in Week 1
- Week 1 must focus entirely on validation — no
  building before confirming someone wants this
- Every action must have a clear, observable outcome
```

## Pro Tips

- **Talk to real people before building anything** — ask 5-10 potential customers about the problem first.
- **Start smaller than you think** — build the smallest version someone would pay for.
- **Do not skip the financial projections** — even rough numbers show if the math works.
- **Revisit this workflow every quarter** — run Steps 2-4 again with updated data.
- **Take the AI's warnings seriously** — it is easy to dismiss concerns about your own idea.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Hurts You | Do This Instead |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Falling in love with the idea | You ignore red flags | Let the market tell you if the idea is good |
| Building before validating | You waste time and money | Spend Week 1 on validation, not building |
| Pricing too low | Hard to raise prices later | Research what competitors charge first |
| Planning forever, never starting | Perfection prevents progress | Follow the 30-day plan |
| Ignoring competitors | You do not know what you are up against | Do proper competitor research in Step 2 |

## Quick Start

1. Do **Step 1** to sharpen your idea
2. Do **Step 2** to check the market
3. Skip to **Step 5** for your 30-day action plan
4. Come back to Steps 3-4 when you need to plan finances
