# Study Workflow

> Turn any subject into organized notes, flashcards, and practice tests — step by step.

---

## Who Is This For?

- Students preparing for exams
- Self-learners picking up a new subject
- Anyone who wants to study smarter, not harder

## What You'll Have at the End

- A clear breakdown of everything you need to learn
- Simple, organized notes for each topic
- Flashcards you can review anytime
- Practice questions with answers
- A day-by-day study schedule

---

## The Workflow

### Step 1 — Map Out What You Need to Learn

Before studying, you need to see the full picture. This step gives you a complete list of everything to cover.

**Copy this prompt and fill in the `[BRACKETS]`:**

```
Role: You are an expert tutor who has taught [SUBJECT] for 20 years.

Objective: Break down everything I need to learn about [SUBJECT] into
a clear, organized list — from the basics to advanced topics.

Context:
- I'm studying for [WHAT — exam name, certification, personal goal]
- My current level is [beginner / intermediate / advanced]
- My deadline is [DATE or "no deadline"]
- I struggle most with [TOPIC or "nothing specific yet"]

Output: Give me a numbered list of all the subtopics I need to study,
organized from foundational → intermediate → advanced. For each
subtopic, add one line explaining why it matters.
```

**After you get the response, ask this follow-up:**

```
What important subtopics are missing from this list that
could show up on an exam or that a beginner often overlooks?
```

---

### Step 2 — Get Clear Notes on Each Topic

Now go through each subtopic one at a time. Don't rush — do one, understand it, then move to the next.

**Copy this prompt for each subtopic:**

```
Role: You are a patient tutor who explains things in plain,
everyday language.

Objective: Teach me [SUBTOPIC] so I can understand it well
enough to explain it to someone else.

Context:
- This is part of my study plan for [SUBJECT]
- My level: [beginner / intermediate / advanced]
- I learn best through [examples / analogies / step-by-step
  explanations / visuals — pick one]

Output: Explain this topic with:
1. A simple definition (1-2 sentences, no jargon)
2. Why it matters (how it connects to the bigger subject)
3. A real-world example I can relate to
4. The #1 mistake people make about this topic
Keep it under 300 words.
```

**Helpful follow-ups you can ask:**

- `Can you explain that like I'm 10 years old?`
- `Give me another example from everyday life.`
- `What's the most common exam question about this topic?`

---

### Step 3 — Make Flashcards

Flashcards force your brain to recall information — which is how you actually remember things.

```
Role: You are a flashcard expert who creates cards that
test real understanding, not just memorization.

Objective: Create flashcards for [SUBTOPIC] that help me
remember and apply what I learned.

Context:
- I just studied [SUBTOPIC] as part of [SUBJECT]
- My exam/goal is [WHAT]
- I want a mix of easy and hard questions

Output: Create 15 flashcards in this exact format:

**Card 1**
Q: [question]
A: [concise answer — max 2 sentences]

Include this mix:
- 5 definition/fact cards (What is...?)
- 5 understanding cards (Why does...? How does...?)
- 5 application cards (What would happen if...?)
```

---

### Step 4 — Test Yourself with Practice Questions

Reading notes feels productive but doesn't build real knowledge. Testing yourself does.

```
Role: You are a professor writing a fair but challenging
practice exam.

Objective: Create a practice test for [SUBJECT/SUBTOPIC]
that covers all the important points.

Context:
- This is for [EXAM NAME / self-assessment]
- Difficulty level: [match my actual exam / slightly harder
  than my exam / beginner friendly]
- Topics to cover: [LIST THE SUBTOPICS or "everything we've
  discussed"]

Output: Create a practice test with:
- 10 multiple choice questions (4 options each, one correct)
- 5 short answer questions (answer should be 2-3 sentences)
- 2 essay questions (for deeper thinking)

Put all answers and explanations in a separate section
at the very end, labeled "ANSWER KEY." For each answer,
briefly explain WHY it's correct.
```

**After completing the test, ask:**

```
I got these wrong: [LIST THE QUESTIONS YOU MISSED].
Explain each one in a simple way and give me a memory
trick to remember the correct answer.
```

---

### Step 5 — Build Your Study Schedule

A plan you follow beats random studying every time.

```
Role: You are a study coach who specializes in efficient
learning and spaced repetition.

Objective: Create a realistic study schedule I can
actually follow.

Context:
- Subject: [SUBJECT]
- Days until exam/deadline: [NUMBER]
- Hours I can study per day: [NUMBER]
- Topics to cover: [LIST FROM STEP 1]
- My weak areas: [TOPICS I STRUGGLED WITH]

Output: Create a day-by-day schedule with:
- Which topic to study each day
- How many minutes to spend on each activity
  (reading, flashcards, practice tests)
- Built-in review days (revisit older material)
- One rest day per week
- A "final review" day before the exam

Format it as a simple table:
| Day | Topic | Activities | Time |
```

## Pro Tips

- **Do one step fully before moving to the next.** Don't skip ahead to flashcards before you understand the notes.
- **Talk back to the AI.** If an explanation is confusing, say *"That didn't make sense. Try explaining it differently."*
- **Study in short blocks.** 25 minutes of focused study + 5 minute break (Pomodoro technique) beats 3 hours of unfocused reading.
- **Review flashcards the same day you make them.** Your memory is strongest right after learning something.
- **Redo practice tests after a few days.** If you still get the same questions wrong, that's where to focus.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Hurts You | Do This Instead |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Not stating your level | AI gives explanations that are too easy or too hard | Always say beginner, intermediate, or advanced |
| Studying everything at once | Nothing sticks when you rush | One subtopic at a time, fully understand it |
| Only reading notes | Passive learning = weak memory | Use flashcards and practice tests actively |
| Skipping the schedule | No structure = procrastination | Build the schedule in Step 5 and follow it |
| Accepting confusing answers | You move forward without understanding | Ask "explain it simpler" or "give me an example" |

---

## Quick Start

Don't have time to read everything? Here's the shortest path:

1. Do **Step 1** to see what you need to learn
2. Do **Step 2** for your weakest topic
3. Do **Step 3** to make flashcards for that topic
4. Repeat Steps 2-3 for each topic
5. Do **Step 4** when you've covered everything
6. Do **Step 5** to plan your remaining study time
